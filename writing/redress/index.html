Redress
2020-06-24 15:22

Based on the Writing Prompt:

You are the guardian of everything that has ever been lost. Anyone may come to you to reclaim what they have lost, but you require them to offer something of significant worth to take its place in your collection.

Redress

Blessings my brethren readily provide. They shower them.
I do not like to bless.
For Ares builds leaders to rule and preside, and seraphim
Are born from her caress.
But bones bear my mark. With suicide the price so grim,
Little is worth redress.

When the sinew had rotted away, it was the mark that kept the skeleton hand clutched around the ledger. When the eyes had receded into their holes, and maggots had long since eaten the tongue, it was the mark that allowed the corpse to read.

And thank the Gods it did read, for I did not come for naught.

It read to me the prayer. The prayer to the Old Gods. The prayer I had recited every morning as a child in school. The prayer I had come to despise.

Ares, Mercury, Mahesh, Osiris. I know all their prayers by heart. But the last one is new. The last one, I’d memorized only recently. The last one, I’d only found in the oldest books, written in arcane script, and burned by the thousands.

From its book, it read to me the prayer to the Oldest God.

I waited for the prayer to end. On the ground I sat, and I waited. I felt the spiders re-building their webs, the webs I’d broken when I’d entered. I felt them on my hands and my hair, but I did not interrupt them. I listened to the prayer, and I waited.

“What have you lost?”

Even now, having come so far, having spurned all that the world offered me, I felt hesitation. I felt it claw at my mind, desperately pleading for me to reconsider. I felt it in my spine as it grew cold and my hair stood on end. I felt it in my chest as my heart shriveled up and died. And I smiled.

“I’ve lost my khushi.”

It did not need explanation.

“Will you pay the price?”

The hesitation was no more. I had killed it. Now there was only desperation.

“Yes.”

And then I saw her in the mist. Adrenaline nulled my hearing. Yearning broke my sense of time. I felt my chest swell as it had only done once before. My feet took me to her, and my hands brought her to me, and as I held her I realized I was weeping.

She is here. She is here. And she, too, is weeping.

“Hush now,” I told her, “there is a boat outside this cave. It will take you home.”

It took more than I had to break from her embrace. My breathing betrayed my fear, and my heart fluttered its dissent, but my chest held high as I sat on the throne.

I grabbed the ledger from the old guardian, and I claimed its mark for myself, and I smiled. For the first time in years, I truly smiled.

“Go now,” I said, “your mother needs you.”
